\generatetitle{Change of basis}
%\chapter{Change of basis}
%\label{chap:rotation.tex}

Given two orthonormal bases
\( \beta_1 = \setlr{ \Be_1, \Be_2, \Be_3 } \), and
\( \beta_2 = \setlr{ \Bf_1, \Bf_2, \Bf_3 } \)

three multivector rotation operators are required to make a change of basis transformation, say

\begin{equation}\label{eqn:rotation:20}
\Bf_k = \Be_k e^{B_k} = e^{-B_k} \Be_k.
\end{equation}

Here \( B_k \) is a bivector for which the orientation is the plane of rotation between \( \Be_k \) and \( \Bf_k \)

\begin{dmath}\label{eqn:rotation:40}
\hat{B}_k = \frac{B_k}{\Abs{B_k}} = \frac{\Be_k \wedge \Bf_k}{\Abs{\Be_k \wedge \Bf_k}},
\end{dmath}

and has the magnitude \( \Abs{B_k} = \theta_k \), the rotation angle between \( \Be_k \) and \( \Bf_k \).

If the coordinate representation in the two bases is given by

\begin{equation}\label{eqn:rotation:60}
\Bx = \sum_k x_k \Be_k = \sum_k y_k \Bf_k,
\end{equation}

then the coordinates can be related by the same transformation.  That is

\begin{dmath}\label{eqn:rotation:80}
y_i
= \Bx \cdot \Bf_i
= \gpgradezero{
\lr{ \sum_k x_k \Be_k } \Bf_i
}
=
\sum_k x_k
\gpgradezero
{
\Be_k \Be_i e^{B_k}
}
=
\sum_k x_k \lr{ (\Be_k \cdot \Be_i) \cosh(B_k) + (\Be_k \wedge \Be_i) \cdot \sinh(B_k)
=
\sum_k x_k \lr{ (\Be_k \cdot \Be_i) \cos(\theta_k) + ((\Be_k \wedge \Be_i) \cdot \hat{B}_k) \sin(\theta_k),
\end{dmath}

However, observe that the bivector dot product factor of the sine is zero

\begin{dmath}\label{eqn:rotation:100}
(\Be_k \wedge \Be_i) \cdot \hat{B}_k
=
(\Be_k \wedge \Be_i) \cdot \frac{\Be_k \wedge \Bf_k}{\Abs{\Be_k \wedge \Bf_k}}
=
\frac{(\Be_k \cdot \Bf_k - \Be_i \cdot \Bf_k) \delta_{ik}}{
\sqrt{ 1 - (\Be_k \cdot \Bf_k)^2 }
= 0,
\end{dmath}

so the change of basis can be expressed as a diagonal rotation matrix with coordinates \( R_{ij} \) given by

\begin{dmath}\label{eqn:rotation:120}
R_{ij}
=
(\Be_i \cdot \Be_j) \cos(\theta_i)
=
\delta_{ij} \cos(\theta_i),
%=
%\cos(\theta_j)
\end{dmath}

and the coordinates are

\begin{dmath}\label{eqn:rotation:140}
y_i = R_{ij} x_j.
\end{dmath}
